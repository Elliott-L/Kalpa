A window into Elyos AI

Elyos AI has built something rare: a real AI product that retains customers, with 30% month-on-month revenue growth, and customers who can point to 80% call automation and 100% increases in out-of-hours bookings. It raised $13M Series A in January 2026. The engineering team is seven people and the system is getting genuinely hard to build. This role is for the engineer who has been constrained by their environment — who wants to own the eval suite, iterate the orchestration engine, train custom models, and see their work in production within days of writing it, alongside a CTO who built a 150-person engineering team from scratch and designed the entire architecture himself.

This is not a features-within-a-defined-system role. The systems are still being invented.

2
Company overview

Elyos AI builds fully autonomous AI agents for trades and field services businesses — plumbers, electricians, gas engineers, fire safety contractors. The agents answer inbound calls, handle outbound follow-up, book and reschedule jobs, take payments, manage engineer dispatch, and operate directly inside the customer's CRM. No human in the loop. Not call deflection. Replacement of the front-office team.

Business model is SaaS at ~£3,000/month per customer (~$50K ACV). Elyos is typically the most expensive software its customers pay for — but more economically valuable than the CRM it integrates with, because it replaces the labour cost of the people who used to operate the CRM.

3
Why this company is interesting

The traction metrics are not projections — they are the current operating reality. Here is what they actually imply:

Strong retention signal
The product is non-optional, not nice-to-have
When customers stay at £3K/month, it means the product has replaced a cost line that doesn't come back. Elyos has eliminated the front-office team for its customers — not improved it. That is a structural dependency, not a feature preference.
30% MoM growth signal
Market is pulling faster than the team can build
This growth rate, sustained over 4–5 months, means demand is real and unit economics work. The constraint is engineering capacity — which is exactly what this hire addresses. This is not growth manufactured by discounting.
Market timing
An underserved industry crossing a threshold
Trades and field services is a £100B+ global market segment. Most businesses are owner-operator SMBs who couldn't previously afford or operate AI infrastructure. The cost/capability curve has moved — Elyos built before the window closed. Competitors will follow.
Defensibility
Proprietary orchestration + per-customer data is a moat
Per-customer eval suites, custom TTS models, and a state machine orchestration engine built for trades workflows are not reproducible by chaining GPT-4 API calls. The longer Elyos operates, the more customer-specific the system becomes.

The voice architecture is cascade (STT → LLM → TTS), not end-to-end real-time models. Panos chose this deliberately: better component control, ability to swap providers, support for custom accent models, and multi-language capability. The decision is revisited continuously as real-time models improve.

The role — deep dive

This is a senior IC founding engineer designation. The work is full lifecycle, customer-proximate, and Python-primary throughout. There is no PM intermediary translating customer requirements — you talk to the customer directly, understand the operational problem, and ship the solution. Several Founding Software Engineer hires are planned as part of a post-Series A engineering wave.

Genuine must-haves
You build things that run in production. You've worked with LLMs beyond the surface level — integrating them into real systems, handling the failure modes, and shipping something users actually depend on.
Python is your primary language. You write it without thinking about it.
You think of yourself as a software engineer who works with AI — not an AI researcher who occasionally writes code.
You're comfortable talking directly to customers — understanding what they actually need, translating it into a technical solution, and owning the outcome without a PM in between.
You have strong opinions about what makes AI systems actually reliable in production — not just what makes a good demo.
You bias toward shipping and iterating. You know when good enough is the right call.
What success looks like
0–3 months: you've shipped something into production, been on-site with a customer during a live deployment, and you understand the system well enough to extend it independently.
3–12 months: you own systems end-to-end. You're making architectural decisions, not just implementing them. The team builds differently because you're in it.
Ongoing: your fingerprints are on the product. Customers notice. The codebase is better than it was when you joined.
Day to day
Building and shipping across the full stack
Python backend, cloud infra, CRM integrations, voice pipeline components. Pull requests go into a codebase running live calls with real businesses every day. The feedback loop is immediate.
Weekly
Direct customer interaction
Working with field services businesses who are not technical. Translating "our agents keep failing on out-of-hours calls during bank holidays" into a specific orchestration fix. This is a core competency, not occasional context.
Sprint
Working directly in customer environments
Every few weeks engineers are on-site with customers — watching the product run in the real world, understanding problems that don't show up in a dev environment, and shipping fixes on the spot. This is how the best improvements get made.
Ongoing
Owning the quality of what you ship
The systems that keep the product reliable are built and maintained by the same engineers who write the features. There's no separate QA layer. You own what you build — and you make it better over time.

Culture & ways of working

This is a company where seniority is earned by shipping, not accumulated by tenure. Decisions are made by whoever has the best understanding of the problem — typically the engineer closest to the customer. There is no process to manage, no roadmap to interpret, no sprint ceremony to protect. The job is to understand the operational problem and build the solution.

The team is physically together in London, full-time. That proximity is deliberate and is a direct input to the product velocity the company has achieved. It is the operating model, not a policy under review.

The culture is small-company serious: no ego, high ownership. AI tools are embraced rather than tolerated — bring your transcript. Panos built a 150-person engineering team and chose to go back to building — that sets the cultural temperature. The people who thrive here are motivated by the quality of the problem and the quality of the team, not by job security, title progression, or process maturity.

Stage 1 — role fit conversation (30 min)
An informal conversation with Nick — no preparation needed and nothing to prove at this stage. It's a chance for both sides to figure out whether there's genuine alignment before either party invests more time.
We'll cover the basics of the role, what you're working on now, and what you're looking for in a next move. You should leave with a clear picture of what the opportunity actually is.
Come with questions. The best conversations at this stage go both ways.
Stage 2 — what the take-home actually involves
Build a streaming CLI chat app in Python or TypeScript with tool calling: a fast weather API (~200ms) and a deliberately slow research API (3–8 seconds)
The APIs have intentional undocumented quirks — discovering and handling them is the core test. You won't be told what they are.
Target: 150–250 lines. Over 400 lines is over-engineering.
AI tools are permitted and expected. You must submit your AI session transcript alongside the code.
Submit a 10–15 min Loom video covering: demo, API discovery process (the most important section), code walkthrough, trade-offs, and self-critique.
The failure case is prompting an LLM to find all the quirks and copy-pasting the output. Every candidate who passes does so because they investigated, reasoned, and owned their decisions.
Stage 3 — on-site session (2.5 hours)
Part 1 — Code discussion (30–45 min)
We review your take-home together — how you implemented key decisions, how you handled edge cases, what you'd do differently.
Expect questions on specific choices: how did you discover a particular API behaviour? Why did you handle it that way? What would you add with more time?
What we're looking for: depth of understanding, investigative thinking, how you respond to feedback.
Part 2 — Pair programming (75 min)
We extend your implementation together — possible directions include real-time audio input, concurrent tool calls, or conversation memory.
You drive, we navigate. You'll encounter unfamiliar APIs. We want to see how you work through them.
What we're looking for: how you approach unfamiliar problems, code quality under time pressure, whether you explain your thinking as you go.
Part 3 — System design discussion (45 min)
A conversation about how to scale the system — not a whiteboard test. We want to understand how you think, not hear perfect answers.
Example threads: how would you handle 100 concurrent voice calls? What breaks first? Walk us through the journey from microphone input to speaker output — where's the latency?
What we're looking for: systems thinking, awareness of operational concerns, ability to reason about trade-offs at scale.
Stage 4 — culture alignment (45–60 min)
A conversation with one of the three founders — Panos, Pip, or Adrian. Less structured than the earlier stages, and deliberately so. This is about understanding how you think, what motivates you, and whether the environment is genuinely the right fit for where you want to go.
Expect questions about how you work, what you've found hard, what kind of problems you're drawn to, and how you operate when things are ambiguous or moving fast.
It's also your opportunity to go deeper on anything about the company, the product, or the team that matters to you before making a decision.

We extend your implementation together — possible directions include real-time audio input, concurrent tool calls, or conversation memory.

A conversation about how to scale the system — not a whiteboard test. We want to understand how you think, not hear perfect answers.
